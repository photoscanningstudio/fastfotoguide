Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

How fast does the Epson FastFoto FF-680W actually scan?
At 300 dpi — the standard quality for viewing and sharing — the FF-680W scans one 4×6 photo per second. That's roughly 1,000 photos per hour when batch-feeding. At 600 dpi (archival quality), it slows to about one photo every 2–3 seconds. Either way, it's dramatically faster than a flatbed or smartphone-based scanning.
Will it damage my old or fragile photos?
The FastFoto uses a gentle roller system designed specifically for photo prints. For extremely delicate, cracked, or torn photos, Epson includes a carrier sheet that encases the photo for protected transport through the scanner. The double-feed detection also prevents two prints from being pulled together, which is a common cause of damage in regular document feeders.
Can it scan film negatives or slides?
No — the FastFoto FF-680W is designed exclusively for photo prints. It cannot scan 35mm negatives, slides, or medium format film. For negatives and slides, you need a dedicated film scanner. Check our film scanner guide above for our current top picks.
What photo sizes does it support?
The FF-680W handles an impressive range: from tiny 2×2 passport photos up to panoramic prints up to 36 inches long. It also handles standard 4×6, 5×7, 8×10, Polaroid, and Instax sizes. Square photos are supported too, making it ideal for Instagram-era prints and older square-format snapshots.
Does it work on Mac and Windows?
Yes — the Epson FastFoto software and ScanSmart software are available for both Windows and Mac. There's also a mobile app (iOS and Android) for basic wireless scanning and slideshow creation. Note: some advanced save-location features are only available on the desktop software, not the mobile app.
Is the FastFoto FF-680W worth the price?
At around $599, it's a significant investment. Our honest take: if you have hundreds or thousands of prints to digitize, it absolutely justifies the cost in time saved. Professional scanning services charge $0.25–$1.00 per photo — so a collection of 2,000 prints could cost you $500–$2,000 to outsource. The scanner pays for itself. If you only have a few dozen photos, consider a flatbed or a phone scanning app instead.
