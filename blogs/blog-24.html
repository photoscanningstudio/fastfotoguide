How to Use Epson FastFoto Software: A Step-by-Step Guide

The FastFoto scanner is only as good as its software. Here's a complete walkthrough of every feature, setting, and workflow tip you need.

The Epson FastFoto FF-680W ships with companion software that handles scanning, file management, color correction, and basic organization. For most users, this bundled software is everything you need to run a complete digitization project — no third-party tools required. But the software has more features than most people discover on their own, and some of the default settings aren't optimal for every situation.

This guide walks through the FastFoto software from installation to advanced features, with practical tips drawn from scanning thousands of photos.

Installation and First Launch

Download the latest version of the FastFoto software from Epson's support website rather than using the disc that shipped with the scanner. The online version is typically more current and includes bug fixes and feature improvements released after manufacturing. The software is available for both Windows and Mac.

During installation, you'll be prompted to connect the scanner via USB or Wi-Fi. For your first session, I strongly recommend USB. It's faster, more stable, and eliminates potential Wi-Fi connection issues that can be distracting when you're learning the software. You can switch to Wi-Fi later once you're comfortable with the workflow.

When the software launches for the first time, you'll see a clean, single-window interface. The main screen is divided into a scanning control panel and a file management area. The scanning panel is where you configure each batch, and the file area shows your completed scans organized by batch.

Configuring Your First Scan

Setting the Save Location

Before scanning anything, set your save destination. Click the folder icon or settings button to choose where scanned files will be stored. Point it at your prepared folder structure — the archive folder on your computer where you've already created decade and event subfolders. You'll change this destination with each batch to direct scans to the correct subfolder.

Choosing Resolution

The software offers 300 DPI and 600 DPI options. For the bulk of a scanning project — standard 4×6 and 5×7 prints destined for digital viewing and standard reprints — select 300 DPI. This delivers one-photo-per-second scanning speed with excellent quality. Switch to 600 DPI only for small prints you plan to enlarge or photos with fine detail worth preserving at maximum resolution. The speed drops to roughly one photo every three seconds at 600 DPI.

Enabling Color Restoration

The auto color restoration feature is one of the FastFoto's most valuable capabilities. When enabled, the software analyzes each scan and applies corrections for age-related fading, color shifts, and contrast loss. The corrected version is saved alongside the raw scan, giving you both versions. Enable this feature for color photos — it produces noticeably better results on faded prints from the 1970s and 1980s with zero manual effort.

For black and white photos, disable color restoration. The algorithm can misinterpret the warm tones of vintage black and white prints as color fading and attempt to "correct" them into neutral gray, which strips the original character from the image.

Enabling Dual-Sided Scanning

This is the feature that separates the FastFoto from every other consumer scanner. When enabled, the scanner captures both the front and back of each photo simultaneously. The back-side scan is saved as a separate file linked to the front by file name — "photo001_front.jpg" and "photo001_back.jpg," for example.

Enable this feature whenever you're scanning photos that might have handwritten notes, dates, names, or stamps on the back. Many old family photos have this kind of information, and it's invaluable for identification and organization. The speed impact is minimal — maybe an extra second per photo — and the metadata you capture is irreplaceable.

Batch Scanning Workflow

With your settings configured, you're ready to scan. Load up to 36 photos into the feeder tray, face-side up. Make sure all photos in the batch are oriented the same way — portrait or landscape — and that they're from the same group you want to file together.

Name the batch in the software. Use descriptive names that match your folder structure: "1985-Christmas" or "1970s-Beach-Vacation." The software appends sequential numbers to each file within the batch, creating filenames like "1985-Christmas-001.jpg" through "1985-Christmas-036.jpg."

Click Scan. The scanner processes the entire batch automatically. While it runs, prepare the next batch — fan the edges of the next set of photos to prevent sticking, confirm their orientation, and decide on the batch name. When the current scan finishes, swap batches and repeat. This overlapping preparation is the key to maintaining a fast, steady pace.

Reviewing and Managing Scans

After each batch, the software displays thumbnail previews of the completed scans. Take 30 seconds to scroll through and spot-check for obvious problems — double-feeds where two photos were scanned as one, skewed alignment, or visible dust streaks. If you catch an issue, rescan the affected photos immediately rather than waiting until the end of the project. It's much easier to find and fix one problem photo while the batch is still fresh in your mind.

The software's built-in viewer is adequate for quick checks but limited for serious editing. For detailed quality review, open files in your operating system's photo viewer or a dedicated application like Windows Photos or Mac Preview. These tools let you zoom to 100 percent and examine fine details that the software's thumbnail view might miss.

Exporting and Organizing Beyond the Software

The FastFoto software saves files directly to the location you specify — there's no intermediate library or database to manage. Your files exist as standard JPEGs in regular folders on your computer, which means you can organize, rename, move, and back them up using any tool you like. This simplicity is a strength — your files aren't locked inside a proprietary system.

For advanced organization, import your scans into Google Photos, Apple Photos, or Adobe Lightroom. These platforms add powerful features like facial recognition, location tagging, keyword search, and batch editing that extend well beyond what the FastFoto software provides. You can also use the scans to create shared family albums — we've covered that topic in detail elsewhere on our homepage.

Troubleshooting Common Issues

Double-Feed Errors

If the scanner pulls two photos through at once, it's usually because the prints were sticking together. Fan the edges of each batch before loading, and reduce the batch size to 20 to 25 photos if double-feeds persist. Very glossy prints are more prone to sticking than matte or semi-gloss finishes.

Streaks in Scans

A recurring streak in the same position across multiple scans indicates dust or debris on the internal glass strip. Open the paper path and gently clean the glass with a lint-free microfiber cloth. This typically resolves the issue immediately.

Color Restoration Over-Correcting

If the auto-correction produces unnatural colors on certain photos — particularly sunset shots or images with intentional warm lighting — you have two options. Disable color restoration for that batch and rely on the raw scan, or use the corrected version as a starting point and fine-tune it manually in an editing application. The raw, uncorrected scan is always preserved alongside the corrected version, so you never lose the original data.

The FastFoto software isn't the most powerful scanning application on the market, but it's purpose-built for its specific task: getting large numbers of family photos scanned quickly and accurately. Master the features covered in this guide, and you'll extract maximum value from both the software and the scanner.
