How to Clean Your Photo Scanner for Best Results

Dust, fingerprints, and debris are the enemies of clean scans. A five-minute cleaning routine keeps your scanner performing at its best.

You're scanning a batch of old family photos, and everything looks great — until you review the files and notice a faint white streak running vertically through every single image in the last fifty scans. That streak is a speck of dust sitting on the scanner's glass or sensor. It's tiny enough that you don't see it with the naked eye, but the scanner's sensor captures it faithfully in every image. Now you have fifty photos to rescan.

This scenario is entirely preventable. Regular cleaning takes five minutes and eliminates the most common cause of scan artifacts. Here's the complete maintenance routine for both flatbed and sheet-fed photo scanners.

Why Scanner Cleaning Matters

Scanners are precision optical instruments. They capture images by shining a light source across the scanning surface and reading the reflected light with a sensor. Any foreign material between the light source, the scanning surface, and the sensor affects the output. A dust particle on the glass creates a dot or streak in every scan. A fingerprint smear creates a blurred, slightly discolored area. A buildup of paper fibers on the feed rollers causes misfeeds and skewed images.

Professional scanning studios like Photo Scanning Studio clean their equipment before every session and sometimes between batches. You don't need to be that rigorous at home, but establishing a basic cleaning routine before each major scanning session prevents the vast majority of quality issues.

Cleaning a Flatbed Scanner

What You Need

A lint-free microfiber cloth — the kind used for eyeglasses or camera lenses. A can of compressed air. Optionally, a small amount of glass cleaner or isopropyl alcohol. That's the entire supply list.

The Cleaning Process

Power off the scanner and unplug it. Open the lid. Use the compressed air to blow loose dust particles off the glass surface. Hold the can upright and use short bursts from about six inches away — don't tilt the can, as this can release liquid propellant that leaves residue.

After blowing off loose debris, fold the microfiber cloth into a small pad and wipe the glass surface in straight, overlapping strokes from one end to the other. Don't use circular motions — they can push debris around rather than removing it. If there are fingerprints or sticky spots that dry wiping doesn't remove, dampen a corner of the cloth with a tiny amount of glass cleaner or isopropyl alcohol. Wipe the spot, then immediately follow with a dry section of the cloth to prevent streaks.

Don't forget the underside of the lid. The white or light-colored pad on the inside of the scanner lid provides the background for your scans. If it's dirty, those marks show up in your scanned images, especially on photos with light-colored borders. Wipe the lid pad with the microfiber cloth as well.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Never use paper towels, tissues, or regular cloth on scanner glass. Paper products leave fibers that create new artifacts. Regular cloth can scratch the glass surface with trapped particles. Never spray cleaning solution directly onto the glass — it can seep into the edges and damage internal components. Always apply solution to the cloth first, and use only a small amount.

Cleaning a Sheet-Fed Scanner

Sheet-fed scanners like the Epson FastFoto FF-680W have internal glass surfaces and rollers that require attention. The process is slightly more involved than cleaning a flatbed, but equally important.

Cleaning the Glass

Sheet-fed scanners have a narrow glass strip inside the paper path where the sensor reads images as they pass by. To access it, open the scanner's paper path cover — most models have a release lever or button that separates the top and bottom halves of the feed mechanism. The glass strip is visible along one edge of the open paper path.

Use compressed air first to remove loose dust, then wipe the glass strip with a lint-free cloth. Be gentle — the glass is narrow and can be awkward to reach. Some scanners include a small cleaning sheet or cloth specifically sized for this purpose. Check your scanner's box for included accessories you might have overlooked during setup.

Cleaning the Rollers

Feed rollers are the rubber or silicone wheels that grip photos and pull them through the scanner. Over time, they accumulate paper dust, oil from the surface of photographs, and tiny fibers. Dirty rollers cause misfeeds, skewed scans, and double-feeds where two photos are pulled through simultaneously.

With the paper path open, locate the rollers. They're small, usually light-colored wheels visible on both the upper and lower halves of the feed mechanism. Dampen a lint-free cloth with a small amount of isopropyl alcohol and wipe each roller while manually rotating it. The cloth will come away visibly dirty, especially if you haven't cleaned the rollers since purchasing the scanner. Continue wiping until the cloth stays clean against the roller surface.

Some scanners include a dedicated roller cleaning kit or cleaning sheets — coated papers that you feed through the scanner to clean the rollers automatically. These work reasonably well for routine maintenance and are less labor-intensive than manual cleaning.

How Often to Clean

For a casual scanning project — maybe a hundred photos over a weekend — clean the scanner once before you start. That's usually sufficient for the entire session. For marathon scanning projects of 500 or more photos, clean every 200 to 300 scans, or whenever you notice artifacts in your output. The old photos themselves shed dust and paper fibers as they pass through the scanner, so the longer you scan without cleaning, the more debris accumulates.

A good practice is to spot-check your scans every 50 images or so. Open a few recent files in full-size view and look for recurring streaks, dots, or smudges that appear in the same position across multiple images. If you see a consistent mark, stop scanning and clean before proceeding. Catching the problem early means fewer rescans.

Preventing Dust in the First Place

The photos themselves are the primary source of debris in the scanner. Old prints stored in dusty boxes carry that dust directly into the feed mechanism. Before scanning a batch of photos, give them a quick wipe with a clean microfiber cloth or fan through the stack to dislodge loose particles. Don't use compressed air directly on old photographs — the force can lift fragile surfaces or push dust deeper into the emulsion.

Your scanning environment matters too. Don't scan in a dusty room or near an open window where airborne particles can settle on the scanner glass between batches. Close the scanner lid or cover when not actively loading photos. These small habits add up to significantly cleaner scans over the course of a long project.

When to Replace Rollers

Scanner rollers are consumable parts. They wear out over time, losing their grip and eventually causing consistent feed problems that cleaning can't fix. Most consumer scanners are rated for tens of thousands of scans before roller replacement is needed, so unless you're running a professional scanning operation, you're unlikely to reach that point. But if you've cleaned the rollers thoroughly and still experience frequent misfeeds, it may be time for replacement rollers — check your scanner manufacturer's website for compatible parts.

Storing Your Scanner Between Projects

If your scanner sits idle between scanning projects — which is the case for most home users — protect it from dust accumulation during storage. Close all covers and trays. If the scanner came with a dust cover, use it. If not, a clean pillowcase draped over the machine works surprisingly well. Store the scanner in a dry, temperature-stable location away from direct sunlight.

When you pull the scanner out for your next project, clean it before the first scan, even if it was clean when you put it away. Months of sitting on a shelf, even covered, can allow enough dust to accumulate to affect scan quality.

A clean scanner produces clean scans. It's the simplest, cheapest, and most impactful thing you can do to ensure your digitized photos look their best. Five minutes of maintenance saves hours of frustration and rescanning.
