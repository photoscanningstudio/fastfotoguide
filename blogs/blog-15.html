Epson FastFoto FF-680W vs. Fujitsu ScanSnap iX1600: Which Wins?

Two excellent scanners, two very different designs. Here's a honest comparison for anyone trying to choose between them.

The Epson FastFoto FF-680W and the Fujitsu ScanSnap iX1600 are both fast, well-built sheet-fed scanners that can handle photographs. They sit in the same price range, they occupy the same shelf space on your desk, and they both promise to make digitization projects faster and easier. But they were designed with different priorities, and choosing the wrong one for your specific needs means either paying for features you don't use or missing features you desperately wish you had.

I've spent extensive time with both machines. Here's the full breakdown.

Design Philosophy: Photo Scanner vs. Document Scanner

This is the fundamental difference that shapes everything else. The Epson FastFoto FF-680W was designed from the ground up as a photo scanner. Every engineering decision — the paper path geometry, the roller materials, the software features — prioritizes the safe handling and optimal capture of photographic prints.

The Fujitsu ScanSnap iX1600 is a document scanner first and a photo scanner second. It was designed to eat through stacks of receipts, invoices, contracts, and business cards at high speed. It can certainly scan photos, and it does so competently, but photographs were not the primary design target.

This distinction doesn't make one scanner objectively better than the other. It makes them better at different things.

Speed Comparison

Both scanners are fast by consumer standards. The FastFoto scans one photo per second at 300 DPI in simplex mode — front side only. With dual-sided scanning enabled to capture notes on the back of photos, speed drops to about one photo every two seconds but still delivers both sides simultaneously.

The ScanSnap iX1600 is rated at 40 pages per minute in duplex mode for letter-sized documents. For standard 4×6 photos, this translates to similar per-photo speeds. In practice, the two scanners are close enough in raw speed that the difference is negligible during a long scanning session. You'll spend more time loading and organizing than you'll ever spend waiting for either machine to scan.

Batch Capacity

The FastFoto's photo feeder holds up to 36 standard 4×6 prints per batch. The ScanSnap's automatic document feeder holds up to 50 sheets, but because photos are thicker than standard paper, the practical capacity for photos drops to around 20 to 30 prints per load. Both numbers are adequate for efficient batch scanning, but the FastFoto's tray was designed to handle multiple photo sizes simultaneously without constant adjustment.

Photo-Specific Features

This is where the FastFoto pulls decisively ahead. It includes automatic color restoration that analyzes each scanned photo and corrects for age-related fading. It offers dedicated photo scanning modes optimized for different print sizes. And its signature feature — simultaneous front-and-back scanning that captures handwritten notes on photo backs — is purpose-built for family photo archives and genealogy projects.

The ScanSnap iX1600 offers none of these photo-specific features. It has excellent document processing capabilities — OCR text recognition, automatic document categorization, one-touch profiles for different document types — but these are irrelevant for photo scanning. When you feed a photo through the ScanSnap, it treats it like any other piece of paper. The scan is clean and accurate, but there's no color restoration, no photo-optimized processing, and no dual-sided photo mode designed for capturing notes.

Document Scanning Capability

If you need to scan documents alongside photos — and many people do, especially when digitizing a loved one's papers along with their photographs — the ScanSnap iX1600 is the stronger document scanner. Its OCR engine is excellent, converting scanned text into searchable, selectable text within PDF files. The ScanSnap Home software organizes scanned documents by type, assigns them to customizable profiles, and integrates with cloud storage services directly.

The FastFoto can technically scan documents too, but it lacks OCR, document categorization, and the ScanSnap's polished document management workflow. If documents are a significant part of your project, the ScanSnap handles that side better.

Build Quality and Paper Path

Both scanners are well-built, but their paper paths differ in ways that matter for photographs. The FastFoto uses a straight-through paper path that minimizes bending — photos enter from the front and exit from the back on a nearly flat trajectory. This is gentler on old, brittle prints and reduces the risk of creasing or cracking.

The ScanSnap uses a U-turn paper path where documents feed in from the top and exit from the top, curving through the scanning mechanism internally. This is standard for document scanners and perfectly fine for paper, but it introduces slightly more bending stress on photographs. For prints in good condition, this isn't a problem. For fragile or elderly prints, the FastFoto's straight path provides an extra margin of safety.

Software Comparison

The Epson FastFoto software is focused and straightforward. It handles scanning, batch naming, and color correction with minimal complexity. You set your resolution, name your batch, choose your save location, and scan. Advanced users might find it limiting, but for the target audience — families digitizing photo collections — the simplicity is a feature.

The ScanSnap Home software is more powerful and more complex. It manages multiple device profiles, organizes scanned items by type, handles cloud integration, and includes business card scanning with contact extraction. This power comes with a steeper learning curve, and many of these features are irrelevant for photo scanning. If you're only scanning photos, the ScanSnap software feels overwrought.

Wi-Fi and Connectivity

Both scanners offer USB and Wi-Fi connectivity. The ScanSnap iX1600 has a slight edge here with its 4.3-inch touchscreen that lets you initiate scans, switch profiles, and manage settings directly on the device without touching your computer. It also supports scanning to multiple computers and cloud services without a direct USB connection.

The FastFoto's Wi-Fi works well for basic wireless scanning but doesn't match the ScanSnap's standalone flexibility. For a dedicated photo scanning project where the scanner sits next to your computer, this difference is academic. For a shared office scanner that multiple people use, the ScanSnap's touchscreen and multi-user support are more useful.

Price Comparison

Both scanners typically sit in the four hundred to five hundred dollar range, making them a similar investment. The ScanSnap occasionally drops lower during sales events due to its broader market and higher volume. The FastFoto holds its price more consistently because it occupies a narrower niche with less competition.

Value depends on your use case. If you're buying a scanner primarily for photos, the FastFoto delivers more relevant features per dollar. If you need a versatile scanner that handles both documents and photos, the ScanSnap gives you broader capability. Both are premium devices that should last years with proper maintenance.

The Verdict

Buy the Epson FastFoto FF-680W if your primary goal is digitizing a family photo collection. The auto color restoration, dual-sided photo scanning, gentle paper path, and photo-optimized software make it the purpose-built tool for this job. No other consumer scanner matches its combination of speed and photo-specific features.

Buy the Fujitsu ScanSnap iX1600 if you need a scanner that handles a mix of documents and photos, if you're digitizing an entire home office, or if you value features like OCR, document categorization, and multi-user Wi-Fi profiles. It's the better general-purpose scanner, and it handles photos well enough for most purposes — just without the photo-specific extras that make the FastFoto special.
