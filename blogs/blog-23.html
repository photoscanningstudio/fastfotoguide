How to Scan and Restore Black and White Photos

Black and white photographs are the most durable prints in your collection — and the most rewarding to restore. Here's how to do both.

Black and white photographs occupy a special place in family archives. They're often the oldest images in the collection — formal portraits from the 1940s, candid snapshots from the 1950s, perhaps even stiff-posed studio photographs from the early 1900s. They carry a weight and gravity that color photos sometimes lack. And from a preservation standpoint, they're remarkably resilient. The metallic silver that forms the image in a black and white print is far more stable than the organic dyes in color photographs, which means many of these prints have survived decades with surprisingly intact detail, even if they look a bit worse for wear on the surface.

Scanning and restoring black and white photos requires a slightly different approach than color work. The tonal range is different. The damage patterns are different. And the aesthetic goals are different — you're not trying to recover lost colors but rather to reveal the full depth and richness of the original silver image.

Scanner Settings for Black and White

Color Mode: Scan in Color, Not Grayscale

This is counterintuitive but important. Even though the photograph is black and white, scan it in full color mode. Here's why. Many "black and white" prints are actually warm-toned or cool-toned — they have subtle brown, sepia, or blue undertones that are part of the print's original character. A grayscale scan strips these tones away, flattening the image into pure neutral gray. A color scan preserves the tonal warmth and gives you more data to work with during restoration.

Additionally, many old black and white prints have developed yellowing, foxing spots, or brown stains over the decades. A color scan captures the exact hue of these artifacts, making them easier to remove selectively in post-processing. In grayscale mode, a yellow stain and a gray shadow look identical, making cleanup harder.

You can always convert a color scan to grayscale later in editing software. You can never add color information back to a grayscale scan. Scan in color, decide later.

Resolution: 600 DPI for Old Prints

Black and white prints, especially those from the 1950s and earlier, are often physically smaller than modern color photos. Cabinet cards, wallet portraits, and small contact prints benefit from 600 DPI scanning to capture enough detail for enlargement. For standard-sized prints in good condition, 300 DPI on a scanner like the Epson FastFoto FF-680W produces excellent results. But for anything smaller than 4×6, step up to 600 DPI.

Common Damage in Black and White Prints

The damage patterns in old black and white photos differ from color photos. Instead of color shifts and fading, you're more likely to encounter silvering, foxing, tears, creases, and surface dirt.

Silvering

Silvering appears as a metallic sheen or mirror-like reflection in the darkest areas of the image. It's caused by silver particles migrating to the surface of the emulsion over time. Silvering is visible when viewing the print at an angle but may not show up in a scan unless the scanner's light source catches it at the wrong angle. If you notice silvering on a print, try scanning it with the print rotated 180 degrees — sometimes a different orientation relative to the scanner's light reduces the artifact.

Foxing

Foxing refers to small brown spots that appear on paper-based photographs and documents over time. It's caused by fungal activity or chemical reactions within the paper fibers. Foxing is primarily a cosmetic issue — it doesn't destroy the image — but it can be distracting in an otherwise clean photograph. Digital removal is straightforward with cloning tools in any image editor.

Restoration Workflow for Black and White Photos

Start with the raw color scan. Open it in your preferred image editor — GIMP, Photoshop, Lightroom, or Vivid-Pix RESTORE all work for this purpose.

Step 1: Crop and Straighten

Remove any border area that isn't part of the photograph. Correct any rotation from the scan — most editing software has a straighten tool that lets you draw a line along what should be a horizontal or vertical edge, and the software rotates the image to match.

Step 2: Remove Surface Damage

Use the clone stamp or healing brush tool to remove foxing spots, dust artifacts, and small scratches. Work at high zoom levels and move methodically across the image. For large tears or missing areas, the content-aware fill features in modern editors can intelligently reconstruct missing portions by analyzing the surrounding image data.

Step 3: Adjust Levels and Contrast

Old prints often lack the full tonal range they originally had. The deepest blacks have faded to dark gray, and the brightest whites have yellowed to cream. Open the Levels or Curves tool and adjust the black point and white point to restore full contrast. This step alone can transform a flat, muddy-looking scan into a print that feels alive and dimensional.

Step 4: Decide on Color Treatment

Now choose whether to keep the warm tones of the original print or convert to neutral grayscale. Both approaches are valid. Keeping the warm tone preserves the artifact's character — it looks like a vintage photograph, which is part of its charm. Converting to neutral grayscale produces a cleaner, more modern look that some people prefer for reprinting or display. There's no wrong answer here; it's a matter of personal taste.

Colorizing Black and White Photos

AI-powered colorization has improved dramatically in recent years. Tools like MyHeritage InColor, DeOldify, and built-in features in some photo apps can add realistic color to black and white photographs automatically. The results can be striking — a somber gray portrait of your grandmother suddenly comes alive with auburn hair and a blue dress.

A word of caution, though. AI colorization is intelligent guesswork. The algorithms assign colors based on patterns learned from millions of images, but they can't know that your grandmother's dress was actually green, not blue. The colors are plausible, not accurate. For family archives, I recommend keeping the original black and white version as the primary file and treating colorized versions as fun supplements, not replacements.

Professional Restoration for Severe Damage

Some black and white photos have damage that goes beyond what automated tools and basic editing can fix. Large missing sections, severe water damage, or photographs that have been folded and creased multiple times may need the skills of a professional restorer. Studios like Photo Scanning Studio can handle scanning and restoration of damaged originals, applying techniques that require experience and artistic judgment that no software can fully replicate.

For the most treasured photos in your collection — a grandparent's wedding portrait, a great-grandparent's only surviving image — professional restoration is worth every penny. These images exist in only one copy, and getting the restoration right preserves them not just for you but for every generation that follows.

Preserving Originals After Scanning

Once your black and white prints are digitized and restored, store the originals in acid-free archival boxes with individual sleeves to prevent surface-to-surface contact. The metallic silver in black and white prints can tarnish where prints touch each other, creating permanent damage. Keep them in a cool, dry location and your original prints will continue to survive alongside their digital counterparts for decades to come.
