How to Scan Old Documents, Letters, and Newspaper Clippings

Your family's story isn't just in photographs. Letters, recipes, clippings, and certificates deserve digital preservation too.

Alongside every family photo collection, there's usually a parallel archive of paper. Handwritten letters from a grandparent who's no longer here. A recipe card in your mother's handwriting, splattered with decades of cooking. A yellowed newspaper clipping announcing a birth, a wedding, or an achievement. These documents carry stories and context that photographs alone can't convey. They're also, in many cases, more fragile than the photos beside them — newspaper degrades faster than photo paper, old letters crumble at the folds, and recipe cards stained with cooking oil become brittle over time.

If you're already digitizing your family's photo collection, adding documents to the project is a natural extension. The same scanner you're using for photos can handle most documents with minor adjustments to your settings and workflow.

Documents Are Not Photos: Key Differences

While the scanning hardware is the same, documents present different challenges than photographs. Photos are printed on relatively sturdy photographic paper with consistent thickness. Documents come in every imaginable paper type — onionskin airmail stationery, thick cardstock certificates, newsprint that's the consistency of tissue paper, and everything in between.

Documents are also more likely to be damaged in ways that complicate scanning. Letters are folded, creating deep creases that don't lay flat on a scanner glass. Newspaper clippings curl and tear at the edges. Certificates and diplomas may have wax seals, embossed stamps, or oversized formats that don't fit standard scanners. Each of these challenges requires a slightly different approach.

Scanning Handwritten Letters

Old letters are often the most emotionally valuable documents in a family collection. A grandparent's handwriting, the stories they chose to tell, the language they used — these details are irreplaceable in a way that even photographs are not. Handle them accordingly.

Resolution and Color Settings

For text-only documents on white or off-white paper, you might assume grayscale scanning is sufficient. For old letters, I recommend scanning in full color at 300 DPI. Here's why. The paper itself is part of the artifact — its color, its texture, its stains all carry historical weight. A grayscale scan loses the warm cream of aged paper, the blue-black of fountain pen ink versus the faded blue of ballpoint, and any decorative elements like colored letterhead or postage stamps on the envelope. Color preserves all of this.

If the letter has very fine handwriting or faded ink that's hard to read, step up to 600 DPI. The extra resolution helps when you zoom in on screen to decipher difficult passages.

Handling Folded Letters

Most old letters have deep fold creases from decades in envelopes. On a flatbed scanner, you can usually lay the letter face-down and press the lid gently to flatten it enough for a good scan. For letters with stubborn folds, place a clean, heavy book on top of the closed scanner lid — the extra weight pushes the paper flatter against the glass.

If a letter is too fragile to press flat, or if the paper has cracked at the fold lines, use a carrier sheet to protect it and scan it through a sheet-fed scanner. The carrier sheet holds the paper flat while guiding it safely through the feed mechanism. Alternatively, scan each half of a folded letter separately and digitally stitch them together in photo editing software.

Scanning Newspaper Clippings

Newspaper is the most fragile paper type you're likely to encounter. Newsprint is made from wood pulp with minimal processing, which means it contains high levels of lignin — the compound that causes paper to yellow and become brittle when exposed to air and light. A newspaper clipping from the 1960s may already be brown, crispy, and ready to crumble if handled carelessly.

Handling Tips

Never feed a fragile newspaper clipping through a sheet-fed scanner without a carrier sheet. The feed rollers can tear the paper instantly. For the safest approach, use a flatbed scanner. Lay the clipping face-down on the glass with extreme gentleness. If the clipping is curled, don't force it flat — place it as flat as it will naturally rest and accept that there may be slight shadows at the curled edges. You can crop or adjust these in post-processing.

Scan newspaper clippings in color at 300 to 600 DPI. Even though newsprint appears black and white, color scanning captures the warm tone of the aged paper and the subtle gray variations that add character and context to the artifact.

Scanning Certificates and Legal Documents

Birth certificates, marriage licenses, military discharge papers, diplomas, and property deeds are not just sentimental — they're legally and historically significant. Scan these at 600 DPI in color to capture every detail, including embossed seals, watermarks, and official stamps that might be difficult to see in lower-resolution scans.

Many of these documents are larger than a standard scanner bed. If you have an oversize certificate that doesn't fit on your flatbed, scan it in two overlapping halves and use photo editing software to merge them. Alternatively, a professional scanning service with large-format equipment can handle oversized documents in a single pass. Photo Scanning Studio handles documents alongside photos and can accommodate non-standard sizes with their professional-grade equipment.

Scanning Recipe Cards and Small Ephemera

Recipe cards, postcards, ticket stubs, greeting cards, and other small ephemera are the most charming items in a family paper collection. They're also the easiest to lose. Digitizing them preserves not just the content but the physical character — the grease stain on Grandma's cookie recipe, the stamp cancellation on a postcard from 1952.

On a flatbed scanner, you can place multiple small items on the glass simultaneously and scan them as a single image, then crop them into individual files later. This batch approach is much faster than scanning each small card individually. Space items about half an inch apart so the auto-crop feature in your scanning software can distinguish between them.

For a sheet-fed scanner, small items can be placed inside carrier sheets and fed through normally. The Epson FastFoto FF-680W handles postcards and recipe cards well when they're in reasonable condition — the thick cardstock actually feeds more reliably than thin paper in many cases.

Organizing Scanned Documents

Create a separate folder structure for documents, distinct from your photo archive. A "Family Documents" top-level folder with subfolders by type works well: "Letters," "Legal Documents," "Recipes," "Newspaper Clippings," "Certificates," and "Miscellaneous." Within the Letters folder, organize by author or recipient and approximate date.

Name your document files descriptively. "1943-Letter-Grandpa-to-Grandma-from-Europe.jpg" tells you everything you need to know at a glance. For newspaper clippings, include the publication name and date if visible: "1975-Buffalo-News-Dads-Bowling-League.jpg."

Preserving the Originals

After scanning, store original documents in acid-free archival folders or sleeves. Never use standard manila folders, plastic sheet protectors made from PVC, or rubber bands. Archival-quality materials prevent further chemical degradation and physical damage. Store documents flat, never folded, in a cool, dry location. If a letter was originally folded, unfold it for storage after scanning — the scan preserves the context of the fold lines without continuing to stress the paper at those points.

Your family's documents tell stories that photographs hint at but can't fully convey. A wartime letter, a baptismal certificate, a recipe in a familiar hand — these are the narrative threads that connect the images in your photo archive to the people and moments they capture. Digitizing them alongside your photos creates a richer, more complete family record that future generations will treasure.
