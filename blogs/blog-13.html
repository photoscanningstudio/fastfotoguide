The Best Way to Back Up Photos to the Cloud

Your scanned photos deserve offsite protection. Here's how to choose a cloud service and set it up so backups happen automatically.

You've scanned your photos. You've backed them up to an external drive. That's two copies — solid progress. But both copies are in the same physical location: your house. A fire, a flood, a break-in, or even an unlucky power surge could take out both simultaneously. The third piece of the 3-2-1 backup puzzle is an offsite copy, and the most practical offsite backup for most people is cloud storage.

Cloud storage means your files live on servers in data centers operated by companies like Google, Apple, Amazon, or Microsoft. These data centers have redundant power, fire suppression, security teams, and copies of your data spread across multiple physical locations. Your photos are safer there than in any single building you own.

What to Look for in a Cloud Backup Service

Not every cloud service is equally suited for a scanned photo archive. Here's what matters most for this specific use case.

Storage Capacity

A typical photo scanning project generates 3 to 15 gigabytes of data depending on the number of photos and resolution settings. Larger projects with 600 DPI scans or combined photo-and-video archives can reach 50 gigabytes or more. You need a service with enough storage to hold your complete archive plus room for future additions. Free tiers from most providers top out at 5 to 15 gigabytes, which works for small collections but falls short for serious scanning projects.

Full-Resolution Preservation

Some services compress uploaded photos to save space. Google Photos, for example, used to offer unlimited storage for photos compressed to 16 megapixels. While 16 megapixels is more than enough resolution for most scanned photos, any compression introduces quality loss. For an archival backup — which is what your scanned collection is — you want a service that preserves files exactly as you upload them, with no resizing or recompression.

Ease of Upload

Uploading thousands of photos manually through a web browser is tedious. The best cloud services offer desktop sync applications that watch a designated folder on your computer and automatically upload any new files. This is the ideal setup for a scanning project: point the sync app at your photo archive folder, and every new scan gets backed up automatically in the background.

Google Photos and Google One

Google Photos is arguably the most popular cloud photo service in the world, and for good reason. Its search and organization features are exceptional — facial recognition, location tagging, and object detection make it easy to find specific images even in a massive collection. The mobile app is polished and the sharing features are excellent for distributing photos to family members.

For backup purposes, Google offers 15 gigabytes of free storage shared across Gmail, Drive, and Photos. Paid plans through Google One start at about two dollars per month for 100 gigabytes and scale up to 2 terabytes for ten dollars per month. Files uploaded in original quality are preserved without compression. The Google Drive desktop app syncs designated folders automatically, making it easy to keep your photo archive continuously backed up.

The Catch

Google's free tier is shared with your email and other Google services. If your Gmail inbox is already large, 15 gigabytes fills up quickly. And Google's pricing for high-capacity plans is not the cheapest option per gigabyte. But the organizational tools — especially the AI-powered search that lets you type things like "birthday cake 1985" and actually find matching photos — add genuine value beyond raw storage.

Amazon Photos

If you're an Amazon Prime member, Amazon Photos deserves serious consideration. Prime subscribers get unlimited full-resolution photo storage — no compression, no quality reduction, no cap on the number of images. This makes it the best value proposition for photo-heavy archives by a wide margin.

The service supports JPEG, PNG, TIFF, and RAW formats, so it can handle scanned photos saved in any common format. There's a desktop app for automatic syncing, and the mobile app includes family sharing features called Family Vault, which lets up to five family members contribute photos to a shared collection.

The limitation is video. Prime members get only 5 gigabytes of free video storage. If your digitization project includes VHS transfers and home movies, you'll need additional storage for those files — either upgrading your Amazon storage plan or using a separate service for video backup.

Apple iCloud

For families deeply embedded in the Apple ecosystem — iPhones, iPads, Macs — iCloud integrates seamlessly with the built-in Photos app. Enable iCloud Photos on any Apple device, and every image in your library syncs automatically to the cloud and to every other connected Apple device.

Apple offers 5 gigabytes free, with paid plans at fifty cents per month for 50 gigabytes, three dollars for 200 gigabytes, and ten dollars for 2 terabytes. The user experience is the smoothest of any cloud service — it genuinely feels like your photos just exist everywhere simultaneously. The downside is platform lock-in. Accessing your photos from a Windows PC or Android device is possible through iCloud.com, but the experience is clunky compared to the native Apple integration.

Backblaze: Set It and Forget It

Backblaze takes a fundamentally different approach. Instead of a photo-specific service, it's a full-computer backup solution. Install the application, and it continuously backs up every file on your computer to Backblaze's servers. Your photo archive, your documents, your downloads — everything is covered.

Pricing is flat: about seven dollars per month per computer for unlimited storage. There are no per-gigabyte charges, no storage caps, and no decisions about which folders to include. If a file is on your hard drive, Backblaze backs it up. For people who want true set-it-and-forget-it peace of mind, Backblaze is hard to beat.

The limitation is that Backblaze doesn't offer photo organization, browsing, or sharing features. It's purely a backup and recovery tool. You'll need a separate service — Google Photos, Apple Photos, or a dedicated app — for actually viewing and organizing your collection.

How to Set Up Automatic Cloud Backup

Regardless of which service you choose, the setup process is similar. Download the service's desktop application. Install it and sign in. Configure it to sync or back up your photo archive folder — the master folder where all your scanned images live. The application will begin uploading your existing files and will automatically upload any new files you add in the future.

Initial uploads can take a long time, especially over residential internet connections. A 10-gigabyte archive on a typical upload speed of 10 megabits per second takes roughly two and a half hours. Larger archives or slower connections can take days. Start the initial upload on a night when you won't need heavy internet bandwidth, and let it run. Subsequent uploads are incremental — only new or changed files transfer — so they're much faster.

Verify Your Backup

After the initial upload completes, verify that your files arrived intact. Log into the cloud service through a web browser, navigate to your photo archive, and spot-check a few images from different folders. Open them full-size and confirm they display correctly. A backup you've never verified isn't a backup you can trust.

Using Multiple Cloud Services

There's nothing wrong with using two cloud services for extra redundancy. Some families keep their primary working collection in Google Photos for its excellent search and sharing features, while running Backblaze in the background for full-computer backup. This gives you two offsite copies plus your local external drive — four copies total, exceeding the 3-2-1 rule with room to spare.

The cost of running two services is modest — roughly fifteen dollars per month combined — and the peace of mind is substantial. For irreplaceable family photos that span decades, over-engineering your backup strategy is a feature, not a flaw.

What About Privacy?

Uploading personal family photos to a cloud service means entrusting them to a third party. Major providers encrypt your data in transit and at rest, and their security infrastructure is generally stronger than anything you'd have at home. That said, if privacy is a paramount concern, services like Backblaze offer client-side encryption options where only you hold the decryption key — even the service provider can't view your files.

For most families, the security provided by mainstream cloud services is more than adequate. The far greater risk to your photos isn't a data breach — it's having no backup at all.
