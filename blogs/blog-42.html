Canon imageFORMULA RS40 Review: A Budget Alternative?

Canon's entry into the photo scanner market promises quality at a lower price point. Does it deliver for family photo digitization projects?

The photo scanner market has been dominated by Epson's FastFoto line for years. The Canon imageFORMULA RS40 represents Canon's attempt to offer a competitive alternative, positioned at a somewhat lower price point with its own set of features and trade-offs. After testing it extensively with a variety of family photo formats, here's the complete picture.

Design and Build

The RS40 is a compact, clean-looking scanner that wouldn't look out of place on a desk or shelf. Canon designed it with a flatbed-style base that doubles as a scanner for single items, plus a sheet-fed mechanism on top for batch scanning standard prints. This dual approach is the RS40's most distinctive feature — you get the gentle, no-contact scanning of a flatbed for delicate items and the speed of a sheet-fed scanner for standard prints, all in one device.

Build quality is solid if not remarkable. The plastic body feels sturdy enough for home use, and the feed mechanism operates smoothly with consistent paper handling. The feeder tray holds up to 20 photos at a time — smaller than the FastFoto's 36-photo capacity but still practical for batch scanning.

Scan Quality

At 300 DPI, the RS40 produces clean, sharp scans with accurate color reproduction. Side-by-side comparisons with the Epson FastFoto show virtually identical image quality on standard 4×6 prints — both scanners resolve fine detail equally well, and color fidelity is comparable. At this resolution, you'd be hard-pressed to identify which scanner produced which file.

At 600 DPI, the RS40 holds its own with excellent detail capture. The optical quality is genuinely good — Canon's experience with imaging sensors shows here. For a scanner at this price point, the image quality exceeds expectations and competes directly with models costing significantly more.

The Flatbed Advantage

The integrated flatbed mode is where the RS40 carves out its niche. Polaroids, thick prints, fragile documents, and photos that shouldn't go through a feed mechanism can all be scanned on the glass surface. You don't need a separate flatbed scanner for special items — the RS40 handles both workflows in one device. For collections with a mix of standard prints and delicate oddities, this versatility is genuinely valuable.

Speed

This is where the RS40 falls behind the Epson FastFoto. Canon rates the RS40 at approximately 30 pages per minute for documents, but real-world photo scanning is slower due to the higher resolution settings and the overhead of saving larger files. In practice, expect about 150 to 200 photos per hour in sheet-fed mode at 300 DPI — respectable, but roughly half the throughput of the FastFoto's 300-plus photos per hour.

For collections under 500 photos, this speed difference is manageable. For thousand-photo projects, it means an extra day or two of scanning time compared to the FastFoto.

Software

Canon bundles its CaptureOnTouch software with the RS40. It's functional but unremarkable — batch naming, resolution selection, save destination, and basic settings are all present. What it lacks is the FastFoto's photo-specific features: automatic color restoration, dedicated photo scanning modes, and dual-sided capture optimized for handwritten notes on photo backs.

The RS40 does support duplex scanning, but it's a standard duplex mode designed for documents rather than a photo-optimized feature. It captures the back of a photo, but without the intelligent pairing and photo-specific processing that makes the FastFoto's dual-sided scanning so useful for family archives.

Who Should Buy the RS40

The Canon RS40 makes sense for families with medium-sized collections of 200 to 500 photos that include a mix of standard prints and non-standard items like Polaroids, thick portraits, and fragile documents. The dual flatbed and sheet-fed design means you don't need two scanners to handle everything in your collection. If your budget is tighter than the FastFoto demands and you don't mind a slower scanning pace, the RS40 delivers good quality at a more accessible price.

Who Should Look Elsewhere

If speed is your primary concern — you have a thousand photos and want them done in a weekend — the Epson FastFoto FF-680W is the better investment. Its one-photo-per-second speed, auto color restoration, and photo-specific software features are purpose-built for large-scale family photo projects. The FastFoto costs more, but for big collections, it saves enough time to justify the premium.

If your collection is very small — under 100 photos — or consists primarily of film negatives and slides, the RS40 may be more scanner than you need. A simple flatbed or a dedicated film scanner would serve those specific needs at a lower cost.

The Verdict

The Canon imageFORMULA RS40 is a capable, well-built scanner that earns its place in the market. It won't dethrone the FastFoto as the speed champion for large photo projects, but it offers something the FastFoto doesn't: a built-in flatbed for delicate and non-standard items. For families who want a single, versatile scanner that handles everything from standard prints to Polaroids to fragile documents at a reasonable price, the RS40 is a smart choice. Just plan for a slower scanning pace and bring your own color correction software to fill the gap left by Canon's more basic bundled software.
