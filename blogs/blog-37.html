Setup Guide

Epson FastFoto FF-680W Setup Guide for Beginners

Just unboxed your FastFoto? Follow this guide and you'll go from sealed box to first scan in under thirty minutes.

The Epson FastFoto FF-680W arrives well-packaged with everything you need to start scanning. But like any piece of technology, the gap between opening the box and actually producing your first beautiful scan can feel wider than it should. Scanner manuals tend to be thick, dry, and written for people who already know what they're doing. This guide is written for you — the person who just wants to scan some family photos and doesn't need a technical manual to do it.

What's in the Box

When you open the packaging, you'll find the scanner itself, a power adapter with a cable, a USB cable for connecting to your computer, a thin setup guide, and a software disc. You may also find protective packing materials inserted into the scanner's feed mechanism — look for orange tape, foam inserts, or plastic shipping locks. Remove all of these before powering on. Leaving packing materials inside can jam the feed mechanism on your first scan.

Step 1: Position the Scanner

Place the scanner on a flat, stable surface with about two feet of clear space behind it. The FastFoto uses a straight-through paper path — photos feed in from the front and exit from the back. If there's a wall immediately behind the scanner, photos will pile up and bend as they exit. A desk, a table, or even a large cutting board on a counter works fine. Just make sure there's room for photos to land cleanly behind the unit.

Step 2: Connect Power and USB

Plug the power adapter into the scanner and into a wall outlet or surge protector. Connect the USB cable between the scanner and your computer. For the initial setup, always use the USB cable even if you plan to use Wi-Fi later — the wired connection is more reliable for driver installation and first-time configuration.

Press the power button on the scanner. You'll hear a brief initialization sound as the scanner warms up and calibrates itself. A steady green light indicates it's ready.

Step 3: Install the Software

Skip the disc that came in the box. Instead, go to Epson's support website and download the latest version of the FastFoto software for your operating system. The online version is always more current than whatever shipped on the disc, and it includes any bug fixes or feature improvements released after manufacturing.

Run the installer. It will detect the scanner connected via USB and install the necessary drivers automatically. Follow the on-screen prompts — the process takes about five minutes. When installation is complete, launch the FastFoto software.

Step 4: Configure Initial Settings

The first time you open the software, take a minute to set your preferred defaults. Choose a save location — the folder on your computer where scanned files will be stored. Set the resolution to 300 DPI for standard scanning. Enable auto color restoration for color photos. Enable dual-sided scanning if your photos have writing on the back.

These defaults apply to every scan session unless you change them. Getting them right now saves you from adjusting settings repeatedly during your first project.

Step 5: Run Your First Test Scan

Select five to ten photos from your collection — a mix of sizes and conditions if possible. This is a test batch, not the start of your full project. You're verifying that the scanner is working correctly, the settings produce good results, and the files save to the right location.

Load the photos into the feeder tray, face side up, aligned to the right edge guide. Adjust the edge guide to snugly fit the width of your photos — not so tight that it bends them, but close enough to keep them straight during feeding. Name the batch something like "Test Batch" and click Scan.

The scanner pulls photos through one at a time, depositing them in the output area behind the unit. Watch the first few photos feed to make sure they're entering straight and exiting cleanly. If a photo skews or double-feeds, stop the scan, remove the jammed photos gently, and try again with fewer photos in the tray.

Step 6: Review Your Test Scans

Navigate to the folder where your test scans were saved. Open each file and examine it at full size. Check for sharpness — text on the photo or fine details like individual blades of grass should be clearly resolved. Check for color accuracy — do the scans look true to the originals, or do they have an obvious color cast? Check for artifacts — streaks, dust spots, or skewed alignment that indicate scanner maintenance is needed.

If the test scans look good, you're ready to begin your actual scanning project. If you notice issues, clean the scanner's glass and rollers before proceeding. We have a detailed cleaning guide elsewhere on our homepage that walks through the process step by step.

Step 7: Set Up Wi-Fi (Optional)

Once you've confirmed everything works over USB, you can optionally configure Wi-Fi. In the FastFoto software, navigate to the scanner settings and look for the wireless configuration option. Select your home Wi-Fi network, enter the password, and test the connection. Once confirmed, you can disconnect the USB cable and scan wirelessly — handy if you want to tuck the scanner away on a shelf and scan from your laptop across the room.

For your first major scanning session, I still recommend USB. It's faster, more stable, and eliminates any network-related variables while you're developing your scanning rhythm. Switch to Wi-Fi once you're comfortable with the workflow and want the convenience of cable-free scanning.

Step 8: Prepare for Your First Real Session

With the scanner set up and tested, you're ready to tackle your photo collection. Before your first serious scanning session, organize your photos into batches, set up your folder structure on the computer, gather your backup drive, and plan your naming convention. Having these elements ready before you start scanning means the actual session flows smoothly — load, scan, name, repeat — without interruptions to figure out logistics.

Keep a microfiber cloth and a can of compressed air near the scanner for periodic cleaning during long sessions. Clean the glass and rollers every 200 to 300 scans, or immediately if you notice streaks in your output.

That's it — you're set up and ready to go. The scanner is tested, the software is configured, and your first batch of scanned photos proves the system works. Everything from here is simply scaling up the same process to cover your entire collection. Enjoy the journey — there's something deeply satisfying about watching decades of family memories transform from fading prints into permanent digital files, one photo per second.
