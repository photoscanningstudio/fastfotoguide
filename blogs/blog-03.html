300 DPI vs 600 DPI: Which Setting Should You Use for Old Photos?

The resolution you choose affects scan speed, file size, and image quality. Here's how to pick the right one — and when it actually matters.

Every photo scanner gives you a choice: scan at a lower resolution and move fast, or crank up the DPI and capture more detail. For people digitizing old family photos, this decision feels weighty. You're handling irreplaceable images. You want to get it right the first time. But the answer isn't always "scan at the highest setting available." Let me explain why.

What DPI Actually Means

DPI stands for dots per inch. When applied to scanning, it describes how many individual points of data the scanner captures per linear inch of your photograph. A 300 DPI scan of a 4×6 inch photo produces an image that is 1,200 by 1,800 pixels — roughly 2.2 megapixels. A 600 DPI scan of the same photo produces 2,400 by 3,600 pixels — about 8.6 megapixels.

More pixels means more detail, but it also means larger file sizes, slower scanning, and higher storage demands. The question isn't which setting captures more — it always will be the higher number — but which setting captures enough for your intended use.

When 300 DPI Is Plenty

For the vast majority of home photo digitization projects, 300 DPI is the right choice. Here's why. A 300 DPI scan of a standard 4×6 print produces an image with enough resolution to display beautifully on any screen — from a phone to a 4K television. It's also sufficient for reprinting at the original size with no visible quality loss.

If your primary goals are preservation, sharing with family members, building digital albums, or creating slideshows, 300 DPI delivers everything you need. The files are manageable in size — typically 2 to 4 megabytes per photo in JPEG format — which means you can store thousands of images on a modest external drive and upload them to cloud services without hitting storage caps quickly.

Speed is the other major advantage. On a scanner like the Epson FastFoto FF-680W, 300 DPI means roughly one photo per second. At 600 DPI, that speed drops to about one photo every three seconds. Over a large project — say, 1,000 photos — that difference adds up to hours of additional scanning time.

The Sweet Spot for Batch Scanning

When you're tackling hundreds or thousands of prints in a weekend project, 300 DPI is the practical sweet spot. You get excellent quality without the bottleneck of enormous files and slow throughput. Professional scanning services commonly use 300 DPI as their standard resolution for exactly this reason.

When 600 DPI Makes Sense

There are specific situations where doubling your resolution is genuinely worthwhile. The most common is when you plan to enlarge a photo significantly. If you want to take a wallet-sized print — those tiny school portraits — and blow it up to an 8×10 or larger, you need the extra pixel data. A 300 DPI scan of a 2×3 inch wallet photo gives you only 600 by 900 pixels, which looks pixelated when enlarged. A 600 DPI scan of the same photo yields 1,200 by 1,800 pixels, which holds up much better at larger print sizes.

The second case is cropping. If you want to zoom in on a specific person or detail within a group photo, higher resolution gives you more room to crop without losing sharpness. A 600 DPI scan lets you crop away half the image and still retain the equivalent of a full 300 DPI capture on the remaining portion.

Small Originals Need Higher DPI

This is the rule most people overlook. DPI matters more when the original print is small. A 4×6 photo at 300 DPI is fine because the physical print is already a decent size. But a 2×3 wallet photo, a passport picture, or a tiny photo booth strip needs 600 DPI or even higher to produce a usable digital file. The smaller the original, the more you need to compensate with resolution.

What About 1200 DPI or Higher?

Some scanners offer 1200 DPI or even 2400 DPI settings. For printed photographs, these ultra-high resolutions produce diminishing returns. Here's why: the paper and ink used in standard consumer photo prints — Kodak, Fuji, drugstore prints — don't contain detail beyond what 600 DPI can capture. Scanning at 1200 DPI doesn't reveal hidden detail; it just captures the grain of the paper at a larger file size.

There are exceptions. Film negatives and slides contain far more detail than prints, and scanning those at 1200 DPI or higher is recommended. If you're scanning negatives, you'll want a dedicated film scanner like the Magnasonic slide and negative film scanner that's designed for high-resolution film capture.

File Size Comparison

Storage matters, especially when you're scanning hundreds or thousands of photos. Here's what to expect for a standard 4×6 print saved as a high-quality JPEG. At 300 DPI, each file runs about 2 to 4 megabytes. At 600 DPI, that jumps to 8 to 15 megabytes per file. At 1200 DPI, files can reach 30 to 50 megabytes each.

For a 1,000-photo project at 300 DPI, you'll need roughly 3 to 4 gigabytes of storage. The same project at 600 DPI requires 10 to 15 gigabytes. That difference matters when you're backing up to a portable SSD or uploading to cloud storage where space is limited or paid.

The Hybrid Approach: Our Recommendation

After scanning thousands of photos across multiple projects, here's the approach I recommend. Scan the bulk of your collection at 300 DPI. This covers standard 4×6 and 5×7 prints destined for digital albums, sharing, and standard reprints. Pull out the special photos — tiny wallet prints, photos you want to enlarge, images with fine detail worth preserving — and scan those at 600 DPI in a separate batch.

This hybrid approach gives you the speed and manageable file sizes of 300 DPI for 90 percent of your collection while capturing maximum detail on the photos that truly deserve it. Label your 600 DPI files or folders clearly so you can distinguish them from the standard scans later.

A Note on Future-Proofing

Some guides recommend scanning everything at the highest possible resolution "just in case." That advice makes sense in theory, but it ignores practical reality. If scanning at 600 DPI means you finish half as many photos before burning out, you haven't preserved more — you've preserved less. A completed 300 DPI scan of your entire collection is infinitely more valuable than a 600 DPI scan of only half of it.

Get every photo scanned first. You can always rescan select images at higher resolution later if the need arises. The originals aren't going anywhere — at least, not in the short term. The bigger risk is never finishing the project at all.

Quick Decision Guide

If you're scanning standard 4×6 or 5×7 prints for sharing and digital backup, use 300 DPI. If you're scanning small prints like wallet photos, passport images, or photo booth strips, use 600 DPI. If you're scanning photos you plan to enlarge to poster size or crop heavily, use 600 DPI. If you're scanning film negatives or slides, use 1200 DPI or higher with a dedicated film scanner. If you're unsure and want to finish the project, start with 300 DPI and scan special images at higher resolution as a second pass.

The best resolution setting is the one that lets you finish the entire project with quality that meets your actual needs. For most families, that's 300 DPI — and that's perfectly fine.
