How to Restore Old Photos Using AI Tools in 2025

Artificial intelligence can now fix scratches, sharpen blurry faces, remove damage, and even add color to black and white photos — often in seconds.

Photo restoration used to require hours of painstaking manual work in Photoshop — cloning out scratches pixel by pixel, rebuilding torn edges, carefully adjusting color channel by channel. Professional restorers still do this for severely damaged images, and their skill remains irreplaceable for complex repairs. But for the everyday damage that affects most scanned family photos — fading, minor scratches, dust artifacts, soft focus, and color shifts — AI-powered tools now produce remarkable results with minimal effort and minimal cost.

The technology has matured dramatically. Where early AI restoration tools produced obviously artificial results with waxy skin textures and hallucinated details, the current generation is genuinely impressive. Faces look natural. Textures are preserved. Colors are plausible. For family archivists processing hundreds of scanned photos, these tools are transformative.

What AI Restoration Can Do

Face Enhancement and Sharpening

Old photos often suffer from soft focus, especially in group shots where the camera struggled with depth of field. AI face enhancement tools analyze blurry facial features and reconstruct them with convincing detail — sharpening eyes, defining jawlines, and recovering expressions that were lost to the limitations of vintage cameras and lenses. Tools like Remini and MyHeritage's Photo Enhancer specialize in this, and the results on moderately blurry faces are often startling.

Scratch and Damage Removal

Scratches, creases, water stains, and foxing spots are common in scanned family photos. AI inpainting tools analyze the surrounding image data and fill in damaged areas with contextually appropriate content. A scratch across a sky gets filled with matching sky tone. A crease through a face gets reconstructed using the undamaged facial features as reference. The results aren't perfect on every photo, but they handle common damage patterns with impressive accuracy.

Colorization

AI colorization applies plausible colors to black and white photographs by analyzing the content and comparing it to millions of color training images. Grass becomes green. Sky becomes blue. Skin tones approximate natural warmth. The technology is impressive but inherently speculative — the AI doesn't know what color your grandmother's dress actually was. Treat colorized images as creative interpretations rather than historical documents. Always preserve the original black and white scan as the authoritative version.

Resolution Upscaling

AI upscaling tools increase the resolution of low-resolution scans by intelligently generating new pixel data based on the existing image. A 300 DPI scan can be upscaled to the equivalent of 600 or even 1200 DPI, enabling enlargements that the original scan resolution wouldn't support. This is particularly useful for small prints — wallet photos, photo booth strips — where the original scan doesn't contain enough pixels for a good enlargement.

Important Limitations of AI Restoration

AI restoration is impressive, but it's not magic. The tools work by making educated guesses about what the original image should look like, based on patterns learned from training data. This means they can introduce artifacts that weren't in the original — smoothed skin textures that look artificial, hallucinated details in heavily damaged areas, or color choices that don't match reality.

For this reason, always keep your original unmodified scan alongside any AI-enhanced version. The original scan is the truthful document — it shows the photo exactly as it existed when scanned. AI-enhanced versions are interpretations that may be more visually pleasing but are not necessarily more accurate. Future AI tools will be better than today's, and having the original scan available means you can re-process it later as the technology improves.

A Practical Workflow for AI Restoration

Start with the best possible scan. AI tools work better with higher-quality input. The Epson FastFoto FF-680W with auto color restoration enabled provides an excellent baseline. Then apply AI tools selectively — not every photo needs enhancement. Focus AI restoration on photos that are important enough to warrant the effort: key family portraits, photos with damaged faces, images you plan to enlarge or print.

For the bulk of a scanned collection, the scanner's built-in auto color correction plus basic adjustments in a tool like Vivid-Pix RESTORE is sufficient. Reserve the heavy AI enhancement — face reconstruction, colorization, extensive damage repair — for the dozen or so most meaningful images in the archive.

AI restoration is a tool, not a replacement for good scanning practices. The better your initial scan, the less restoration is needed, and the better the AI results will be when you do apply them. Start with quality input, enhance selectively, and always preserve the originals.
