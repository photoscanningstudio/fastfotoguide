Google Photos vs. iCloud vs. Amazon Photos: Best for Scanned Photos?

Three major cloud services, three different strengths. Here's which one fits your scanned photo archive best.

Once your family photos are scanned and organized on your computer, the next step is getting them into the cloud. An offsite backup protects your archive from local disasters — fires, floods, theft, and hardware failure. But which cloud service deserves your family's memories? Google Photos, Apple iCloud, and Amazon Photos all want to be your photo home, and each makes a compelling case with a different combination of storage, features, and pricing.

For scanned photo archives specifically, the priorities are slightly different than for everyday phone photography. You need reliable storage for files that may be larger than typical phone photos. You want organizational tools that work well with images that lack GPS metadata and camera EXIF data. And you need sharing features that accommodate family members of all ages and technical abilities.

Google Photos: Best for Search and Organization

Google Photos wins on intelligence. Its AI-powered search is genuinely remarkable — you can type natural language queries like "beach," "birthday," "red car," or even "two people hugging" and get accurate results from your scanned archive. The facial recognition groups photos of the same person together across decades, which is extraordinary when applied to a family collection spanning the 1960s through the 2000s.

The free tier offers 15 gigabytes shared across Google services. Paid Google One plans start at about two dollars per month for 100 gigabytes, scaling to 2 terabytes for ten dollars per month. Photos upload at original quality with no compression on paid plans. The desktop uploader and mobile apps sync designated folders automatically.

Scanned Photo Strengths

Google Photos handles scanned images well despite the absence of camera metadata. It indexes visual content rather than relying on EXIF data, so its search works even when photos lack date, time, and location information. The auto-enhance feature applies tasteful improvements to scanned images with a single click. Album creation and sharing are intuitive, and shared albums support comments from family members.

Limitations

The 15 GB free tier fills quickly if you also use Gmail heavily. Cross-platform experience is excellent but requires a Google account for full functionality. Privacy-conscious users may be uncomfortable with Google's AI analyzing their family photos, though Google states it does not use Photos content for advertising purposes.

Apple iCloud: Best for Apple Families

If your family lives in the Apple ecosystem — iPhones, iPads, Macs — iCloud provides the most seamless photo experience available. Enable iCloud Photos on one device, and your entire library syncs to every other Apple device automatically. Scanned photos uploaded from your Mac appear on your iPhone within minutes.

Apple's on-device AI offers facial recognition, object detection, and memory curation similar to Google's, but all processing happens on your device rather than in the cloud. This provides a meaningful privacy advantage for families who care about where their data is analyzed. The Shared Photo Library feature lets up to six family members contribute to and browse a unified collection.

Scanned Photo Strengths

The integration with Apple Photos on Mac makes importing scanned photos effortless — drag files into the app and they sync everywhere. The Memories feature creates curated slideshows from your library that can surface wonderful forgotten images. The sharing experience for Apple-to-Apple households is unmatched in smoothness.

Limitations

The free tier is a meager 5 gigabytes — barely enough for a modest scanned collection. Paid plans start at fifty cents per month for 50 GB. The cross-platform experience is weak — Android users and Windows-only family members access iCloud through a clunky web interface. If your family spans Apple and non-Apple devices, iCloud creates a divided experience.

Amazon Photos: Best Value for Prime Members

Amazon Photos offers the most storage for the money — if you're already a Prime subscriber. Prime members get unlimited full-resolution photo storage at no additional cost. Unlimited. No compression, no quality reduction, no per-photo charges. For a family with a massive scanned collection — ten thousand photos at 600 DPI — this unlimited storage is an extraordinary value.

The Family Vault feature lets you invite up to five family members to contribute to a shared space, each with their own unlimited photo storage. The service supports JPEG, PNG, TIFF, and RAW formats, covering every file type a scanning project might produce.

Scanned Photo Strengths

The unlimited storage removes all capacity anxiety. You can upload your entire archive at full resolution without worrying about hitting limits or paying overage fees. The desktop app syncs folders automatically. Print ordering is integrated directly into the platform.

Limitations

Amazon Photos' search and organizational tools are less sophisticated than Google's. Facial recognition works but isn't as accurate. The interface feels more utilitarian than either Google Photos or Apple Photos. Video storage is limited to 5 GB for Prime members — a constraint if your archive includes digitized home movies alongside photos. And the unlimited storage disappears if you cancel Prime.

Our Recommendation by Situation

If your family uses a mix of devices and you want the best search and organization tools, choose Google Photos. Its AI-powered search is the best way to navigate a large scanned archive, and its cross-platform support ensures everyone in the family can access the collection regardless of their device.

If your family is all-Apple and you value privacy and seamless integration, choose iCloud. The experience within the Apple ecosystem is unmatched, and on-device AI processing keeps your family's faces and memories off third-party servers.

If you're already a Prime member and your archive is very large, choose Amazon Photos. Unlimited full-resolution storage is an unbeatable value for serious photo archivists, and the Family Vault feature extends that generosity to the whole household.

There's nothing wrong with using two services. Many families use Google Photos for daily browsing and sharing while running Amazon Photos as a backup archive. The cost of maintaining two cloud services is modest, and the redundancy provides extra protection for irreplaceable family memories.
