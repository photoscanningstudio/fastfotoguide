How to Scan Photos on Wi-Fi Using the Epson FastFoto App

Cut the cable and scan from anywhere in your home. Here's how to set up wireless scanning and when it makes sense over USB.

The "W" in the Epson FastFoto FF-680W stands for wireless, and it's one of the features that elevates this scanner above a simple utility device. Wi-Fi connectivity means the scanner doesn't have to sit next to your computer. You can place it on a shelf in another room, in a closet, on a dining room table during a scanning marathon — anywhere within range of your home Wi-Fi network. Your computer, tablet, or phone connects to it wirelessly, and scanned files transfer over the network automatically.

That said, wireless scanning involves some trade-offs compared to a direct USB connection. Understanding when to use each connection method is the key to getting the most out of the FF-680W's flexibility.

Setting Up Wi-Fi on the FastFoto

The initial Wi-Fi setup is easiest when done through a USB connection first. Connect the scanner to your computer with the included USB cable, then open the Epson FastFoto software. In the scanner settings or setup menu, look for the Wi-Fi configuration option. The software walks you through selecting your home Wi-Fi network, entering the password, and testing the connection.

Once the scanner is connected to your network, it remembers the credentials. You can disconnect the USB cable and the scanner will automatically connect to your Wi-Fi network whenever it's powered on. A small indicator light on the scanner confirms active Wi-Fi connectivity.

Alternative: Wi-Fi Direct

If you don't have a Wi-Fi router or prefer a direct connection, the FastFoto supports Wi-Fi Direct mode. This creates a peer-to-peer wireless connection between the scanner and a single device — no router required. It's useful for scanning in locations without Wi-Fi infrastructure, like a relative's house or a storage unit where you're scanning photos on-site.

Wi-Fi Direct is slightly slower than router-based Wi-Fi and only connects to one device at a time, but it eliminates dependency on external network infrastructure. Enable it through the scanner's button panel or the software settings.

Using the Mobile App

Epson offers a companion mobile app — Epson Smart Panel — available for both iOS and Android. The app connects to the scanner over Wi-Fi and provides a streamlined scanning interface optimized for phone and tablet screens. You can initiate scans, preview results, adjust basic settings, and save files directly to your mobile device's photo library.

The mobile app is best for quick, casual scanning sessions — a few photos here and there, rather than marathon batch scanning projects. The smaller screen makes it harder to manage batch names, folder structures, and quality review compared to the full desktop software. Think of the app as a convenience tool for ad hoc scanning and the desktop software as your production workhorse.

Scanning to Your Phone's Photo Library

One particularly useful workflow: scan photos using the mobile app and save them directly to your phone's camera roll. From there, they automatically sync to whatever cloud service your phone uses — Google Photos, iCloud, or Amazon Photos. This means a photo goes from the scanner to the cloud in one smooth chain without ever touching your computer. For family members who primarily manage photos on their phone, this can be the simplest possible workflow.

Wi-Fi vs. USB: When to Use Each

Use USB For:

Marathon scanning sessions where speed matters. USB transfers are faster than Wi-Fi, and the connection is rock-solid — no dropped connections, no latency spikes, no interference from neighboring networks. If you're scanning 500 or more photos in a single session, USB is the more reliable and efficient choice.

Also use USB for the initial setup and any firmware updates, which typically require a direct wired connection.

Use Wi-Fi For:

Occasional scanning — a few dozen photos at a time. When you want the scanner tucked away on a shelf rather than taking up desk space next to your laptop. When scanning at a location away from your usual workstation. When you want to scan directly to a phone or tablet rather than a computer.

Wi-Fi scanning is also excellent for scanning to a NAS drive on your network. The scanner saves files directly to the network storage without routing through your computer at all, which is convenient if your NAS is your primary archive destination.

Troubleshooting Common Wi-Fi Issues

Scanner Not Found on Network

If your computer or phone can't detect the scanner, check that both devices are on the same Wi-Fi network. Dual-band routers broadcast separate 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz networks — the scanner may connect to one while your device connects to the other. Most scanners use 2.4 GHz, so make sure your computer or phone is also connected to the 2.4 GHz band during setup.

If the scanner still isn't visible, power-cycle it and your router. Many network connection issues resolve with a simple restart of both devices. If problems persist, try reassigning the Wi-Fi credentials through a temporary USB connection.

Slow Transfer Speeds

Wi-Fi scanning can feel sluggish if your network is congested. Other devices streaming video, downloading large files, or running online games compete for bandwidth with scan transfers. If you notice slow file saves during scanning, consider pausing bandwidth-heavy activities on other devices, or switch to USB for the remainder of the session.

Distance from the router also affects speed. If the scanner is far from the router — particularly through walls or floors — signal strength drops and transfer speeds slow. Moving the scanner closer to the router or adding a Wi-Fi extender can help.

Intermittent Disconnections

Some routers aggressively manage idle connections, disconnecting devices that haven't communicated in a while. If the scanner drops off the network between batches, check your router's settings for any "idle timeout" or "automatic disconnection" features and disable them for the scanner's IP address. Alternatively, keep the scanning software open and active — the regular communication between software and scanner prevents idle disconnections.

The Best of Both Worlds

The beauty of the FF-680W's dual connectivity is that you don't have to choose permanently. Use USB when you're at your desk for a big scanning session. Use Wi-Fi when you want to scan a handful of photos from the couch or send scans directly to your phone. The scanner remembers its Wi-Fi credentials and USB works immediately when plugged in — switching between modes takes seconds.

For families where multiple people want to scan photos on different devices, Wi-Fi is the clear winner. Everyone can take turns scanning from their own laptop or phone without swapping cables, and each person can save directly to their preferred location. The scanner becomes a shared household resource rather than a single-user peripheral.
