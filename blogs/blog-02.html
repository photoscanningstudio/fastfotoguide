The Best Photo Scanner of 2025: Epson FastFoto FF-680W Full Review

After scanning over 3,000 family photos, here's our honest verdict on the scanner that changed the game for home digitization.

When Epson introduced the FastFoto line, it solved a problem that had frustrated families for years: the sheer tedium of scanning old photographs one at a time. The Epson FastFoto FF-680W is the latest and most refined model in that lineup, and after spending several months with it — scanning everything from pristine 4×6 glossies to crumbling Polaroids — I'm confident in calling it the best photo scanner you can buy in 2025 for home use. But it's not flawless. Let me walk you through exactly what it does well, where it stumbles, and who should buy it.

What Makes the FF-680W Different

Most scanners fall into two camps. Flatbed scanners give you excellent quality but agonizing speed — one photo at a time, 30 to 60 seconds each. Standard document scanners are fast but designed for paper, not photographs, and they can scratch delicate prints. The FastFoto FF-680W sits in a category almost by itself: a high-speed sheet-fed scanner built specifically for photographs.

The headline specification is speed. Epson rates it at one photo per second at 300 DPI. In practice, the actual scanning is that fast, though there's overhead time for each batch while the software saves files and resets the feeder. Realistically, expect to scan about 250 to 350 photos per hour in a steady working session, which still makes it roughly ten times faster than a flatbed.

Build Quality and Design

The FF-680W is compact for what it does — about the size of a large toaster oven. The matte charcoal body looks professional without being flashy. The paper path is gentle, which matters when you're feeding irreplaceable family photographs through the mechanism. Epson uses a straight-through design that minimizes bending, reducing the risk of creasing or cracking old prints.

The photo feeder tray holds up to 36 standard 4×6 prints at once. It's worth noting that this number drops if you're scanning larger prints — 5×7s or 8×10s — since the tray capacity is based on physical thickness, not count. In testing, I found that loading 25 to 30 photos per batch was the sweet spot for avoiding double-feeds without sacrificing too much speed.

Scan Quality at 300 and 600 DPI

At 300 DPI, which is the setting you'll use for the vast majority of a large scanning project, the FF-680W produces clean, accurate reproductions. Colors are faithful to the original print, and there's enough detail to make a sharp 8×10 reprint from a 4×6 scan. Fine details like individual blades of grass or text on a birthday cake come through clearly.

Switching to 600 DPI doubles the resolution and roughly quadruples the file size. The quality improvement is visible when you zoom to 100 percent on screen, but for most practical purposes — sharing digitally, standard reprints, slideshow presentations — the difference between 300 and 600 DPI is marginal. I recommend using 600 DPI selectively, for photos you know you'll want to enlarge or crop significantly.

Auto Color Restoration

One of the FF-680W's most useful features is built-in color restoration. Old photos fade unevenly — reds go first, then yellows, leaving behind a bluish or greenish cast. The FastFoto software analyzes each scan and attempts to reverse that fading automatically. In my testing, it worked remarkably well on moderately faded prints from the 1970s and 1980s. Severely faded photos from the 1960s needed additional manual correction using dedicated restoration software like Vivid-Pix RESTORE, but the auto-correction provided a solid starting point.

The Software Experience

Epson's FastFoto software handles scanning, file naming, color correction, and basic organization. It runs on both Windows and Mac, and the interface is straightforward. You name your batch, choose your resolution, and hit scan. Files save as JPEGs to a folder you specify.

The software's strongest feature is dual-sided scanning. If your photos have handwritten notes, dates, or names on the back — and many vintage prints do — the FF-680W captures both sides simultaneously. The back-side scan is saved as a separate file linked to the front image by file name. For anyone doing genealogy research or building a family archive, this is an enormously valuable feature that you won't find on most competing scanners.

Where the software falls short is organization. It gives you batch-level folder creation and sequential file naming, but there's no tagging, no date recognition, and no search functionality. For serious organization, you'll want to export your scans into a dedicated tool like Google Photos, Apple Photos, or Adobe Lightroom.

Wi-Fi and Connectivity

The "W" in FF-680W stands for wireless, and the scanner supports both USB and Wi-Fi connections. USB is faster and more reliable for marathon scanning sessions, which is what I'd recommend for a big project. Wi-Fi is convenient for occasional one-off scans — you can keep the scanner on a shelf and connect to it from your laptop anywhere in the house.

There's also a companion mobile app that lets you scan directly to your phone or tablet. It works, but the smaller screen makes it harder to manage batch settings and quality-check results. I'd treat the app as a nice bonus rather than a primary workflow tool.

What It Does Not Do Well

No product is perfect. The FF-680W struggles with very thick prints — those old-school double-weight Kodak prints from professional studios sometimes cause feed errors. Heavily curled photos can also jam if you don't gently flatten them first or use carrier sheets. And the color restoration, while good, occasionally misjudges white balance on photos with intentional color casts, like sunset shots where it tries to "correct" the warm tones into neutral.

The scanner is also not designed for slides or negatives. If you have boxes of 35mm slides, you'll need a dedicated film scanner like the Magnasonic slide and negative scanner for those formats.

Who Should Buy the Epson FastFoto FF-680W

If you have more than 200 loose photo prints to digitize, this scanner pays for itself in time savings alone. Families tackling generational photo collections, genealogy researchers building digital archives, estate organizers preserving a loved one's memories, and small businesses digitizing client photos for restoration services will all find the FF-680W indispensable.

If you only have a few dozen photos, a smartphone scanning app might be adequate. But once the volume crosses into the hundreds, a sheet-fed scanner isn't a luxury — it's the only tool that makes the project realistically completable.

The Bottom Line

The Epson FastFoto FF-680W remains the gold standard for home photo scanning in 2025. It's fast, reliable, and purpose-built for the specific task of digitizing printed photographs. The auto color restoration genuinely works, the dual-sided scanning is a rare and valuable feature, and the build quality inspires confidence when you're feeding irreplaceable originals through the machine.

It's not cheap. But when you consider the alternative — paying a professional scanning service two to four dollars per photo — the FF-680W breaks even after about 200 scans. Everything beyond that is pure savings. More importantly, your photos never leave your house, and you control the quality from start to finish.
